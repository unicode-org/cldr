# Researching Common Usage

CLDR data aligns to the most common usage in the market, which may not align with what a language authority prescribes. For most locales this is straightforward, but languages that have a limited digital presence or are not standardized, demonstrating common usage can be challenging. This guide provides practical methods for finding and documenting evidence to support your contributions.

See [Requesting Changes](/requesting_changes) for general documentation guidelines.

## Where to Find Evidence

 **Note:** Avoid using examples that might be programmatically generated. For example, the date at the top of an article or a table of currency values might have been generated by software (often CLDR), and might differ from what someone would type in manually.
 
### Published and Printed Media

- **Newspapers, magazines, and books** in your language (print or online) are strong evidence. Look at how dates, numbers, currency symbols, and names are formatted.
- **Government publications**, official forms, and legal documents often reflect established conventions.
- **Textbooks and educational materials** show widely accepted usage, especially for number formats, date formats, and script conventions.
- **Packaging, signage, and other printed materials** are also useful — product labels, street signs, and store displays often reflect everyday usage. For example, [CLDR-17624](https://unicode-org.atlassian.net/browse/CLDR-17624) used images of cereal box packaging as evidence in a discussion about the German sharp S (ẞ).
- **Style guides** published by language authorities, news organizations, or governments reflect agreed-upon conventions and carry significant weight.

### Digital Sources

- **Websites** that serve your language community. News sites, community portals, blogs, and local government sites are good sources.
- **Social media** can show how people actually write in everyday contexts. Look at patterns across many users, not just one person's preference.
- **Mobile apps and software** already localized in your language. Check how dates, numbers, and currency are displayed — these reflect existing conventions.
- **Wikipedia** in your language often follows community-agreed conventions for formatting.
- **Video media** such as YouTube videos can be useful when they show on-screen text (for example, a video discussing prices with currency amounts displayed). Be cautious: verify that the content is human-authored and not machine-translated or generated by an AI tool, since such content may itself reflect CLDR data and create a circular reference.

### Community and Oral Sources

- **Language community organizations** may have style guides or formatting conventions.
- **Discussions with language speakers** from different regions can help identify the most common usage when written sources are limited. Document these conversations when referencing them.
- **Radio and television broadcasts** may use on-screen text or published schedules that show formatting conventions.
- **Google Maps Street View** can be a valuable source for observing real-world usage in signage and storefronts. For example, when deciding between commas or periods as the decimal symbol for regional Spanish variants, CLDR contributors have walked along city streets in Street View looking at gas station price signs to see which symbol is used.

## What to Document

When submitting a request for a data change in JIRA or proposing a new value in the Survey Tool, provide as much supporting evidence as possible. Stronger evidence leads to faster resolution.

Good evidence includes:

- **Screenshots and links** — every claim must include a screenshot and a link to the source. We need to be able to see the evidence and trace the history of the data.
- **Multiple independent sources** — a single example is not enough. Show that the usage is consistent across different sources.
- **Prominence of the source** — evidence from a major newspaper or government site carries more weight than a personal blog. If the source may not be familiar to CLDR TC reviewers (for example, a regional newspaper or a local government portal), briefly explain what it is and why it is authoritative for your language community.
- **Alternatives** — if more than one convention exists (for example, different currency symbols or date formats), document all of them and indicate which is more common. Failing to mention known alternatives significantly reduces credibility and may lead to the ticket being put on hold until someone has time to research the proposed change more deeply.

## Examples of Well-Researched Tickets

[CLDR-5321](https://unicode-org.atlassian.net/browse/CLDR-5321) is a good example: it covers a number format change for Khmer and includes an attached PDF that documents the conventions, cross-references community feedback, and addresses both the thousands and decimal separators — making it straightforward to review and resolve.

[CLDR-19146](https://unicode-org.atlassian.net/browse/CLDR-19146) is another excellent example focusing on linguistic fallback logic for Kirundi (rn). The ticket justifies shifting the primary fallback from English to French by citing national education policies (UNICEF), government portal standards, and CIA World Factbook data. By aligning technical fallback rules with the sociolinguistic reality of Burundi, it provides a clear, data-backed rationale that simplifies the approval process for maintainers.

## Challenges with Standardization

National languages have often gone through an institutionalized process standardizing the language. Disadvantaged communities have higher dialect and personal variance. While its most pronounced in speech (not applicable to CLDR), it often manifests in spelling and word choice.

Notably, the "standard" form and the "common usage" are not always identical — for example in English "hitherto" technically means "until now" and by dictionary standards used to be considered the standard form. Common usage would use "until now" or "up to this point".

Many languages are actively going through a standardization process. For example Romansh in Switzerland has 5 major regions with distinct conventions and deciding a single Standard Romansh was a difficult process. Currently its been unified as "Rumantsch Grischun" but it is not universally accepted as community members favor their home dialects.

### Regional Variants

When it comes to regional variation it's usually straightforward if there are clear differences between countries. For example, "color" for en_US and "colour" for en_GB. However some places may use both versions, like in India / en_IN — prefer the majority usage.

CLDR currently does not have the capacity to handle within-country variation using subdivision tags. For example, Valencian is considered a variant of Catalan. The Valencian Community has the tag `esvc` and in BCP-47 the locale code be expressed as `ca-ES-sd-esvc` but that isn't currently supported. In the case of Valencian though there is a variant tag registered in IANA `valencia` so CLDR uses `ca-ES-valencia`.

### Variant Subtags

To help differentiate the dialects the Romansch community has registered the various dialects as IANA Variant Tags to the [IANA Language Subtag Registry](https://www.iana.org/assignments/language-subtag-registry/language-subtag-registry), so they can be expressed in technical systems, e.g. `rm_CH_rumgr` for Rumantsch Grischun.

Information to register variant tags can be seen on the IANA website. Once a variant tag is accepted by IANA it can be added to the CLDR systems.

> **Note:** Even with variant data in CLDR, the language variant tag extension is not consistently supported across all technical platforms right now — but it's a Catch-22: we need more interest and submissions before more software platforms will support them.

### Revisionist Contributions

Please do **not** misrepresent your community and submit revisionist spellings when submitting to CLDR. For languages with few digital resources it can be tempting to change the spelling and word conventions to match ideal forms of the language. For example, someone contributing to English could try to revive the letter Þ (thorn) by submitting translations like "Þursday" for "Thursday".

In an ideal world that makes sense and change has to come from somewhere, but in practice adopting revisionist spellings would hurt a community's ability to read their own language. This could easily backfire and cause community members to avoid using their language in digital systems because it's no longer their language.

Over time, if new spelling conventions are adopted more widely by the community we can consider moving the standard version to the new reform. Until then, we recommend proponents of a revision to contribute common usage data instead so the community is best supported. Once the common usage data is available, register an IANA variant for their revision and submit that data separately.

## Tips for DDL Contributors

- **Start with what you have.** Even limited evidence is better than none. A few clear examples from prominent sources can be enough.
- **Be honest about gaps.** If evidence is scarce, say so. The community appreciates transparency.
- **Use the forum.** The Survey Tool forum lets you discuss with other vetters. Share your evidence and reasoning there to build consensus.
- **Combine sources.** A mix of print, digital, and community sources is stronger than relying on one type alone.
- **Ask for help.** If you are unsure how to document usage, reach out through the [DDL Support](https://unicode.org/cldr/ddl-support) form.
